  What each variant brings, and how they differ

  Variant A ‚ÄĒ Merkle, sorted node-sets public. The diagnostic baseline. It's the
  first thing you build after flat to "buy parallelism," and it's where the
  binding tax appears in its rawest form: the partition is disclosed in
  plaintext (the sorted node-sets + endpoints are public inputs), the verifier
  does K+1 bb verify plus four equality cross-checks. Its virtues are real and
  worth stating plainly: it's the cheapest on total gates at every measured K,
  its partition check is deterministic (a sort ‚ÄĒ no Schwartz‚ÄďZippel soundness
  error), and it's the simplest to reason about. Its defect is the unconditional
  leak and an O(N) glue memory floor (~159 MB ‚ÄĒ the glue sorts all N nodes
  regardless of K), which is what pins its per-node memory at K=8.

  Variant A++ ‚ÄĒ grand product + in-circuit Fiat-Shamir. The correction here is
  important and the docs get it right: A++ is not a cheaper A. Glue-to-glue it
  crushes A (6,987 vs 14,822 gates, 31 vs 159 MB at K=2) because it replaces the
  O(N) sort with an O(K) grand-product multiset check. But it relocates that
  O(N) work into the K segments (+~3.5%/segment), so on totals it's strictly 
  more expensive than A at every K. So if you judge A++ as a standalone
  hierarchical point, it looks dominated. Its actual justification is threefold
  and you should rank it by durability:
  1. It's the circuit you recurse on. Its public surface is 9 fields, 
  M-independent, which is why a single in-circuit verification is 704,363 gates 
  flat at both N=8 and N=480. An A-inner exposes M+4 fields, so the recursive
  outer grows with segment size (and the measured outer prove time is ~21s vs
  ~16s ‚ÄĒ A++-inner is the better bridge).
  2. High-K memory: the O(K) glue has no O(N) floor, so A++'s per-node memory
  keeps falling past K=8 where A plateaus.
  3. Distributed partition check: O(M)/segment parallel + O(K) merge, vs A's
  serial O(N) sort.

  So A++'s case is entirely forward-looking: it earns its keep only because 
  recursion stays in the thesis. Cut recursion and A++ becomes a curiosity.

  committed-A / committed-A++ ‚ÄĒ bind on hiding commitments. This is the cure for
  the binding tax's privacy symptom without paying recursion's 704k√óK. Each
  segment exposes a blinded Poseidon fold C_i instead of plaintext partition
  data; the glue takes the values + openings as witness and does the partition
  checks in-circuit; the verifier just checks sub_i.C_i == glue.C_i (opaque
  blobs ‚Üí the bookkeeping becomes ZK for free). Public surface collapses to
  {root, T, C_0..C_{K-1}}. The crucial implemented finding: committed-A and
  committed-A++ land on the same privacy rung (multiset hidden computationally,
  interior order info-theoretically gone, K revealed). They differ only in glue
  cost/mechanism ‚ÄĒ committed-A keeps the O(N) sort + O(N) commit-fold,
  committed-A++ keeps the distributed grand-product + O(K) commit-fold. So
  they're an ablation pair, not two independent frontier points: committed-A++
  is the one that's both the high-K/recursion-bridge point and
  privacy-equalized; committed-A is "the honest cure for A."

  The dependency isn't real at the level you're presenting

  The thing to separate is recursion-the-concept from

  of the route is valid, treated as a black box. "Decompose into K segments,

  Recommended order (keeps your preferred shape, turns the wart into a payoff)

  idealized/black-box inner ("a proof of one segment"). Run the dilemma: at

  A++. The inner-circuit choice becomes the climax, not a buried prerequisite ‚ÄĒ
